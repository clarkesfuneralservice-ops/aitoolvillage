If you've ever copy-pasted information between two apps, manually forwarded an email to your team, or typed the same data into a spreadsheet and a CRM, you've done a job that Zapier automation can do for you automatically — in seconds, every single time, without you lifting a finger again.

This guide walks through exactly how to build your first Zapier automation, from understanding the basic concept to publishing a working Zap, plus five real examples you can adapt for your own business today.

What is a Zapier Automation?

A Zapier automation — called a "Zap" — connects two or more apps so that when something happens in one app, Zapier automatically performs an action in another. The classic example: someone fills out a contact form on your website, and Zapier automatically adds them to your CRM, sends them a welcome email and notifies your sales team in Slack — all instantly, with zero manual work.

Every Zap is built from two core components:

  • Trigger — the event that starts the automation (a new form submission, a new email, a new row in a spreadsheet)
  • Action — what happens automatically as a result (create a contact, send a message, update a record)

Zapier connects to 6,000+ apps, which means almost any two tools your business uses can be connected without custom development work.

Before You Start

You'll need a free Zapier account (no credit card required) and accounts for whichever two apps you want to connect. For your first automation, pick something simple — connecting a form tool to your email provider, or a spreadsheet to a messaging app — rather than starting with a complex multi-step workflow.

💡 Beginner tip: Start with a single trigger and a single action. Once you're comfortable with how Zapier works, you can add more action steps to build more sophisticated automations.

Step-by-Step: Building Your First Zap

1

Create a new Zap

From your Zapier dashboard, click "Create Zap." This opens the Zap editor where you'll build your automation.

2

Choose your trigger app and event

Search for the app that should start your automation (e.g. Google Forms, Typeform, Gmail) and select the specific event — such as "New Form Response" or "New Email Matching Search."

3

Connect your trigger account

Zapier will ask you to log in to the trigger app and authorise the connection. This is a one-time step per app.

4

Add your action app and event

Search for the app where you want something to happen (e.g. HubSpot, Slack, Google Sheets) and select the action — such as "Create Contact" or "Send Channel Message."

5

Map your data fields

Tell Zapier which piece of data from your trigger should populate each field in your action — for example, mapping the form's "email" field to the CRM's "email" field. Zapier shows live sample data to make this intuitive.

6

Test your Zap

Click "Test step" to run your automation with real or sample data. Check that the action completed correctly in the destination app before moving on.

7

Publish and turn it on

Once your test succeeds, publish the Zap and toggle it on. It will now run automatically every time your trigger event occurs — no further action needed from you.

Building Automations with AI

Zapier's AI workflow builder removes most of the manual setup above. Instead of selecting apps and mapping fields step by step, you describe what you want in plain English — for example: "When someone fills out my contact form, add them to HubSpot and send me a Slack notification" — and Zapier's AI generates the complete automation automatically, including the field mapping.

This has made Zapier automation significantly more accessible to non-technical users, cutting the time to build a working Zap from 10-15 minutes down to under a minute for straightforward automations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the test step — always test before publishing, since a broken field mapping can send incorrect data into your business tools at scale.
  • Building overly complex Zaps too early — start simple with single-step automations before attempting multi-step workflows with conditional logic.
  • Not monitoring task usage — each action step consumes a task; multi-step Zaps with high trigger frequency can use up your monthly task allowance faster than expected.
  • Forgetting to handle errors — Zapier's paid plans include auto-replay and error notifications; without these, a failed automation may go unnoticed.
