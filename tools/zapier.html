What is Zapier Automation?

Zapier automation is the process of connecting two or more apps so that an action in one app automatically triggers an action in another — without writing code or manually transferring data. Zapier is the platform that makes this possible, connecting 6,000+ apps including Gmail, Slack, Salesforce, Shopify, QuickBooks and Google Sheets.

Each individual automation in Zapier is called a "Zap." A Zap consists of a trigger (the event that starts the automation, like "new form submission") and one or more actions (what happens next, like "create CRM contact" and "send Slack notification"). Once published, a Zap runs automatically in the background every time the trigger event occurs — no manual intervention needed.

Zapier's AI features have expanded what automation can do. AI Actions let tools like ChatGPT and Claude trigger real actions inside your connected apps — turning conversational AI into an execution layer for your business workflows, not just a chat interface.

How Zapier Automation Works — Step by Step

Building a Zapier automation follows the same basic process regardless of complexity:

1
Choose your trigger app and event — the app and specific event that starts the automation, such as "New Row in Google Sheets" or "New Lead in Facebook Ads."
2
Choose your action app(s) and event(s) — what should happen automatically, such as "Create Contact in HubSpot" or "Send Message in Slack." Multi-step Zaps can chain several actions from one trigger.
3
Map the data fields — tell Zapier which piece of information from the trigger (e.g. the lead's email) should populate each field in the action (e.g. the CRM's email field).
4
Test the Zap — Zapier runs a live test using real or sample data so you can confirm the automation works correctly before publishing.
5
Publish and monitor — once turned on, the Zap runs automatically every time the trigger event occurs. Zapier's dashboard shows a history of every automation run for monitoring and troubleshooting.
Example automation: A new lead fills out a form on your website → Zapier automatically adds them to your CRM, sends a personalised welcome email via Gmail, and posts a notification in your sales team's Slack channel — all within seconds, with zero manual work.

Building automations with AI

Zapier's AI workflow builder removes even the manual setup step — describe what you want in plain English ("when someone fills out my contact form, add them to HubSpot and notify me in Slack") and Zapier's AI generates the complete Zap automatically, including field mapping. This has made Zapier automation accessible to non-technical users who previously needed a developer to set up integrations.

Frequently Asked Questions
Zapier automation is the process of connecting two or more apps so that an action in one app automatically triggers an action in another, without manual effort or code. For example, a new form submission can automatically create a CRM contact, send a welcome email and notify your team in Slack — all instantly and without you touching anything.
Choose a trigger app and event (what starts the automation), choose one or more action apps and events (what happens next), map the relevant data fields between them, test the Zap with real or sample data, then publish it. Zapier's AI workflow builder can also generate a complete automation from a plain English description, skipping the manual setup steps entirely.
Zapier's free plan includes 100 automation tasks per month and 5 active single-step Zaps — enough for basic, low-volume automations. Multi-step Zaps, premium app integrations and higher task volumes require a paid plan starting at $19.99/month (Starter plan, 750 tasks/month).
A task is counted each time a Zap successfully performs an action step. A simple Zap with one trigger and one action uses 1 task per run. A multi-step Zap with three actions uses 3 tasks per run. Understanding task usage matters for choosing the right plan — high-volume multi-step automations consume tasks faster than simple single-action Zaps.
Zapier is better for simplicity and app coverage — it's easier to set up and has more pre-built integrations, making it the better starting point for non-technical users. Make (formerly Integromat) is better for complex, high-volume automations at lower cost per operation — its visual workflow builder handles branching logic more elegantly. Most businesses start with Zapier for ease of use and consider switching to Make if automation volume grows significantly.
