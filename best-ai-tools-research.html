Common questions
Frequently Asked Questions
Perplexity AI is the best all-round AI research tool — it searches the web in real time, cites every source and gives synthesised answers. For academic research specifically, Elicit is better as it searches scientific papers and extracts key findings. For analysing documents you already have, Claude or Google NotebookLM are the top choices.
Several excellent free options exist: Perplexity AI (free plan), Research Rabbit (completely free), Semantic Scholar (completely free) and Google NotebookLM (free). For academic literature review, Elicit's free plan handles basic research well. Most researchers use a combination — Perplexity for quick answers, Research Rabbit for literature discovery and Zotero for reference management.
AI research tools don't replace Google Scholar but significantly improve on it. Tools like Elicit, Consensus and Semantic Scholar are all better than Google Scholar for finding and understanding relevant research quickly. They extract key findings, show consensus across papers and surface related work automatically — tasks that take hours manually in Google Scholar.
Using AI tools to find, organise and summarise research is generally accepted — it's analogous to using a database or search engine. Where ethics become important is in writing: passing off AI-generated text as your own original analysis is considered academic dishonesty at most institutions. The tools listed here are primarily research assistance tools, not writing generators — they help you find and understand sources, which is always acceptable.
Professional researchers commonly use Perplexity for quick web research, Elicit and Semantic Scholar for literature discovery, Scite for citation quality assessment, Research Rabbit for mapping research landscapes and Claude or ChatGPT for synthesising and summarising findings. Zotero remains the standard for reference management across disciplines.
